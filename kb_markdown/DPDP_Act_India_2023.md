# Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDP), India 2023

**Source:** Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY), Government of India
**Reference:** Act No. 22 of 2023, The Gazette of India, August 11, 2023
**Official URL:** https://www.meity.gov.in/data-protection-framework
**Gazette Notification:** https://egazette.gov.in/WriteReadData/2023/248045.pdf
**Commenced:** Phased implementation commencing 2025 (see "Phased Implementation Timeline" section below for details; specific dates are per government notifications and should be verified on the MeitY website)
**Last Reviewed:** March 2026

## Overview

The **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act)** is India's first comprehensive data protection legislation. It was passed by Lok Sabha on August 7, 2023, by Rajya Sabha on August 9, 2023, and received Presidential assent on August 11, 2023.

The Act provides for the processing of **digital personal data** in a manner that recognizes:
- The **right of individuals** (Data Principals) to protect their personal data
- The **need to process** such personal data for lawful purposes

The DPDP Act applies to:
- Processing of digital personal data **within India** where data is collected online or digitized from offline sources
- Processing of digital personal data **outside India** if it relates to offering goods/services to individuals in India

The Act establishes the **Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)** as the adjudicatory body for data protection disputes.

## Key Rules

### 1. Key Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Data Principal** | The individual to whom the personal data relates (equivalent to GDPR's "data subject") |
| **Data Fiduciary** | Any person/entity who determines the purpose and means of processing (equivalent to GDPR's "controller") |
| **Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF)** | A Data Fiduciary designated by the Government based on volume/sensitivity of data processed |
| **Data Processor** | Any person who processes data on behalf of a Data Fiduciary |
| **Consent Manager** | A person registered with the Board who acts as a single point of contact for the Data Principal to manage consent |
| **Digital Personal Data** | Personal data that is in digital form, or personal data collected in non-digital form and subsequently digitized |

### 2. Consent Requirements (Sections 5–9)
- Processing of personal data requires **free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous consent** of the Data Principal
- Consent must be obtained through a **clear and plain language notice** before or at the time of data collection
- The notice must be available in **all 22 languages** listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Indian Constitution
- Data Principals have the **right to withdraw consent** at any time (with the same ease as giving consent)
- **Consent Managers** may be appointed to manage consent on behalf of Data Principals
- Processing without consent is permitted for **"legitimate uses"** including:
  - Specified purpose for which data was voluntarily provided
  - State functions (subsidies, benefits, services, licenses, permits)
  - Medical emergencies
  - Employment purposes
  - Public interest (epidemic response, law enforcement cooperation)

### 3. Obligations of Data Fiduciaries (Sections 8–10)
- **Purpose limitation** — Process data only for the purpose for which consent was obtained
- **Data accuracy** — Ensure completeness, accuracy, and consistency of personal data
- **Data retention** — Erase personal data when the purpose is fulfilled and retention is no longer necessary (unless required by law)
- **Security safeguards** — Implement reasonable security measures to prevent personal data breaches
- **Breach notification** — Notify the **Data Protection Board** and **affected Data Principals** of any personal data breach
- **Grievance redressal** — Publish contact details of a Data Protection Officer (or equivalent) and establish a grievance mechanism

### 4. Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF) — Enhanced Obligations (Section 10)
Entities designated as SDFs must additionally:
- Appoint a **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** based in India
- Appoint an **independent data auditor**
- Conduct **periodic Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)**
- Conduct **periodic audits** of data processing activities
- Comply with any additional requirements prescribed by the Government

### 5. Rights of Data Principals (Section 11–14)
| Right | Description |
|---|---|
| **Right to Access** | Request a summary of personal data being processed and details of processing activities |
| **Right to Correction and Erasure** | Request correction of inaccurate data or erasure of data no longer required |
| **Right to Withdraw Consent** | Withdraw consent at any time; consequences of withdrawal to be borne by the Data Principal |
| **Right to Grievance Redressal** | File complaints with the Data Fiduciary; if unresolved, escalate to the Data Protection Board |
| **Right to Nominate** | Nominate a person to exercise rights in case of death or incapacity |

### 6. Cross-Border Data Transfers (Section 16)
- Personal data may be transferred **outside India to any country** EXCEPT those specifically **restricted by the Central Government**
- The Government may notify a **"negative list"** of countries to which transfers are prohibited
- This is a **permissive framework** — unlike GDPR, transfers are allowed unless specifically blocked
- As of March 2026, the Government has not yet notified the negative list

### 7. Penalties (Section 33, Schedule)
| Contravention | Maximum Penalty |
|---|---|
| Failure to take reasonable security safeguards resulting in a personal data breach | **₹250 crore (~$30 million)** |
| Failure to notify the Board and Data Principals of a breach | **₹200 crore (~$24 million)** |
| Non-fulfillment of obligations for children's data | **₹200 crore (~$24 million)** |
| Non-fulfillment of additional obligations by Significant Data Fiduciaries | **₹150 crore (~$18 million)** |
| Breach of any other provision of the Act | **₹50 crore (~$6 million)** |
| Breach of duties by Data Principals (false complaints, false information) | **₹10,000** |

### 8. Children's Data (Section 9)
- **Verifiable parental consent** required before processing data of children (under 18)
- **No tracking, behavioral monitoring, or targeted advertising** directed at children
- **No processing detrimental to the well-being** of children
- Government may prescribe age verification mechanisms
- Certain categories of Data Fiduciaries (e.g., healthcare, education) may be exempted from parental consent

## Risks

### Compliance Risk Assessment
| Risk Area | Description | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| **Consent Management** | Failure to obtain valid consent or manage consent lifecycle | Critical |
| **Breach Notification** | Not notifying DPBI and Data Principals within required timelines | Critical |
| **Data Security** | Inadequate security measures leading to data breaches — up to ₹250 crore penalty | Critical |
| **Children's Data** | Processing children's data without parental consent or with targeted advertising | High |
| **Cross-Border Transfer** | Transferring data to a future "negative list" country | Medium (evolving) |
| **Retention** | Retaining personal data beyond the purpose fulfillment + legal retention period | High |
| **SDF Non-Compliance** | Not meeting enhanced obligations (DPO, audits, DPIA) when designated as SDF | High |
| **Grievance Response** | Not responding to Data Principal requests within prescribed timelines | Medium |

### Comparison with GDPR — Key Differences
| Aspect | DPDP Act 2023 | GDPR |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Digital personal data only | All personal data (digital and non-digital) |
| **Sensitive Data** | No special category distinction | Distinguishes "special categories" (health, biometrics, religion, etc.) |
| **Legal Bases** | Consent + legitimate uses (limited) | 6 legal bases including legitimate interests, contractual necessity |
| **Cross-Border Transfers** | Allowed unless restricted (negative list) | Restricted unless adequacy/safeguards in place |
| **Right to Portability** | Not included | Included |
| **Right to Object** | Not included | Included |
| **DPO Requirement** | Only for Significant Data Fiduciaries | For public bodies and large-scale processing |
| **Maximum Penalty** | ₹250 crore (~$30M) | €20M or 4% global turnover |
| **Supervisory Authority** | Data Protection Board of India | National Data Protection Authorities |

## Mitigations

### Implementation Roadmap
**Phase 1: Assessment (Immediate)**
1. Conduct a **data inventory** — Map all digital personal data processed, categories, sources, and processing purposes
2. Identify if the organization may be designated as a **Significant Data Fiduciary**
3. Assess current **consent mechanisms** against DPDP requirements
4. Review **data breach response** capabilities and notification processes

**Phase 2: Compliance Framework (0–6 months)**
1. **Consent management system** — Implement/update to support:
   - Granular consent collection with clear purpose specification
   - Multi-language consent notices (22 scheduled languages)
   - Easy consent withdrawal mechanism
   - Consent audit trail
2. **Data Protection Officer** appointment (if SDF)
3. **Data breach response plan** — Include DPBI notification workflow
4. **Privacy notices** — Update for DPDP Act requirements
5. **Vendor/processor agreements** — Include DPDP Act obligations

**Phase 3: Operational Compliance (6–12 months)**
1. **Data retention policy** — Implement automated purge for data no longer needed
2. **Data Principal rights management** — System for handling access, correction, erasure requests
3. **Security measures** — Implement encryption, access controls, monitoring aligned with "reasonable security safeguards"
4. **Children's data controls** — Age verification and parental consent mechanisms
5. **Training** — Organization-wide awareness program on DPDP Act obligations

**Phase 4: Ongoing (Continuous)**
1. **Periodic DPIA** (for SDFs)
2. **Annual data protection audit** (for SDFs)
3. **Cross-border transfer monitoring** — Watch for negative list notifications
4. **Regulatory monitoring** — Track DPBI guidance, rules, and enforcement actions

## Key Regulatory Body

**Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)**
- Established under Section 18 of the DPDP Act
- Functions as an **adjudicatory body** (not a regulator)
- Determines non-compliance and imposes penalties
- Hears complaints from Data Principals
- Digital-first approach — proceedings conducted electronically
- Decisions appealable to the **Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT)**
