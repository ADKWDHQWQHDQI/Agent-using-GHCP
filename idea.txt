 Compliance Compass – RAG Agent Overview
Compliance Compass is a text-only Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) agent built using Microsoft AI Foundry.
The purpose of the agent is to help compliance and risk teams quickly evaluate regulatory risks when working with vendors, handling sensitive data, or operating across jurisdictions.
Instead of generating answers purely from the model, the system retrieves relevant policy snippets from a lightweight Knowledge Base (KB) and then reasons over them to produce grounded and traceable responses.
The agent takes a user query describing a scenario, retrieves the most relevant compliance policies, analyzes the risks, and produces a clear, structured report with recommended actions.
1. Knowledge Base Documents
The Knowledge Base used by Compliance Compass is intentionally lightweight.
It contains around 10–12 structured Markdown files, each about 5–10KB in size. These documents are stored in a vector database (Postgres with pgvector) so that relevant information can be retrieved quickly (usually in under one second).
Instead of storing entire PDFs, the KB contains summarized and structured excerpts of compliance policies. Each document is formatted with consistent sections such as:
Overview
Key Rules
Risks
Mitigations
This structure helps the retrieval system find the most relevant information efficiently.
Below are examples of documents included in the KB, mainly focused on fintech and manufacturing compliance scenarios:
RBI_Data_Localization_2018_Guidelines.md
Covers RBI requirements that payment and customer data must be stored in India.
RBI_Cross_Border_Transactions_Circular_2023.md
Explains forex regulations and restrictions related to international vendors.
GDPR_Article_44_Transfers.md
Provides guidance on transferring personal data outside the EU.
GDPR_Schrems_II_Post_2020.md
Explains the invalidation of Privacy Shield and the need for Standard Contractual Clauses.
DPDP_Act_India_2023.md
India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act covering consent requirements, breach reporting, and potential fines up to ₹250 crore.
RBI_Vendor_Onboarding_Risk_2024.md
RBI rules for evaluating third-party vendors, especially those providing technology services.
EU_China_Data_Flows_2024.md
Information about data transfers between EU and China.
SEBI_Insider_Trading_Policy.md
Compliance requirements related to financial disclosures.
Export_Control_US_EU_India_2025.md
Export restrictions for sensitive technologies.
Mitigation_Templates_DPA.md
Example clauses for Data Processing Agreements.
Incident_Response_Best_Practices.md
Steps organizations should follow during data breaches.
Risk_Scoring_Framework.md
A template used to calculate compliance risk scores.
Together, these documents cover most common regulatory questions related to data protection, vendor risks, and cross-border operations.
2. Sources of the Documents
All Knowledge Base documents are created from public and official regulatory sources.
No proprietary or confidential data is used.
The documents are typically downloaded or referenced from official websites and then converted into Markdown format for easier processing.
Some of the main sources include:
Reserve Bank of India (RBI)
https://www.rbi.org.in
Provides circulars and master directions related to financial regulations.
Examples:
Payment Data Localization rules
Third-party vendor oversight guidelines

Contains official GDPR regulations and cross-border transfer rules.
Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY)
https://www.meity.gov.in
Official source for the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023).
Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)
https://www.sebi.gov.in
Regulations for financial market compliance and insider trading.

https://edpb.europa.eu
Guidelines and interpretations related to GDPR enforcement.
US Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS)
https://www.bis.doc.gov
Export control rules related to technology transfers.
These sources are periodically reviewed to ensure the Knowledge Base reflects the latest regulatory updates.
3. User Prompts
Users interact with Compliance Compass by describing real-world compliance scenarios in natural language.
These prompts usually resemble short emails or internal notes written by compliance teams. The goal is to describe the situation clearly so the agent can retrieve relevant policies.
Typical prompts are 1–3 sentences long and may include details such as vendor location, data type, or regulatory jurisdiction.
Some example prompts include:
Vendor Risk Assessment
"Assessing risks for a new AI analytics vendor based in Singapore that will process our customer transaction data. Are there any RBI localization concerns?"
Cross-Border Data Transfer
"We are exporting manufacturing technology to the EU. Can you check if there are GDPR transfer safeguards or potential compliance risks?"
Contract Review
"Review this contract clause: 'Customer data stored on servers in China.' Are there privacy risks?"
Scenario Simulation
"If we onboard a US vendor after Schrems II, what safeguards should we implement for personal data transfers?"
Follow-up Analysis
"Based on the previous assessment, estimate the cost of compliance audits."
The agent extracts key entities from the prompt, such as country, data type, vendor relationship, and regulatory context, which helps refine document retrieval.
4. Agent Output
After retrieving relevant information, Compliance Compass generates a structured compliance report.
The report combines retrieved policy snippets with reasoning to produce a clear explanation of risks and recommended actions.
The output is designed to be easy to scan and practical for decision-making.
Typical characteristics of the output:
200–400 words
Structured sections
Risk score
Actionable recommendations
Clear traceability to policy sources
Example Output:
Compliance Compass Report
Scenario: China-Based AI Vendor Handling Customer Data
Generated: March 10, 2026
Confidence: High
Risk Overview
Risk Score: 8 / 10 (High)
The main risks come from data localization requirements and cross-border transfer restrictions.
RBI Requirements
RBI mandates that payment and customer data must be stored in India. While processing abroad is allowed in some cases, a copy of the data must remain within India.
GDPR Cross-Border Transfers
China is not considered an adequate jurisdiction under GDPR. Data transfers require safeguards such as Standard Contractual Clauses and Transfer Impact Assessments.
Retrieved Knowledge Base References
RBI Data Localization Guidelines
"No restriction on processing abroad, but payment data must be stored in India."
GDPR Article 44
"Transfers to non-adequate countries require safeguards such as SCCs or binding corporate rules."
Recommended Actions
Immediate Actions
Conduct a Transfer Impact Assessment for the vendor.
Add a data residency clause requiring Indian data storage.
Medium-Term Actions
Perform a vendor cybersecurity audit.
Implement monitoring and reporting aligned with RBI compliance guidelines.
Escalation Option
If the vendor cannot meet localization requirements, consider shifting the deployment to India or Singapore-based infrastructure.
The report is returned in Markdown format, which can be displayed in applications such as Streamlit dashboards and exported to PDF or Excel for documentation.
